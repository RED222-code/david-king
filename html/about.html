My Approach

Design is not decoration. It is strategy.

The way I think about software is different from most developers. I do not see design and engineering as separate disciplines. I see them as the same problem.

01

Identity before implementation

Before I write a single line of code, I think about what this product should feel like. What impression should it leave? How should it move? What should make it instantly recognisable? A product with a strong identity does not need to compete on features alone — it competes on feeling.

02

Experience over implementation

Most developers build what works. I build what works and what stays with people. Micro-interactions, motion, hierarchy, contrast — none of it is cosmetic. It is all signal. A well-crafted interface communicates competence before a user has read a single word.

03

Scalability is built in, not bolted on

I write code that does not need to be rewritten six months later. Clean architecture, sensible abstractions, and a data model that can grow without fracturing. Scalability is not a feature you add — it is a discipline you bring from the start.

04

Differentiation as the deliverable

My aim is never to deliver software that meets a brief. My aim is to deliver something that gives a client a genuine competitive edge. When your product feels more considered, more alive, and more intentional than the alternatives — that is not subjective. That is value.

"Software that only functions is forgettable. Software that creates a feeling becomes indispensable."

Beyond Code

What else shapes how I think.

The way I approach software is shaped by interests far outside of it. Each one has made me a more precise, more imaginative, and more complete developer.

01

Cybersecurity

Security is not a feature — it is a foundation. My interest in cybersecurity means I think about attack surfaces, data exposure, and trust models from the beginning of a build, not as an afterthought. I build software that is designed to be hard to break.

02

Storytelling

Every interface tells a story. Good storytelling — the kind that pulls people in and keeps them there — is the same skill as good UX design. My interest in narrative directly informs how I structure user journeys, write copy, and sequence product experiences.

03

Game Development

Games are the hardest product to build well. They demand that every element — mechanics, feedback, pacing, visual language — work together seamlessly. Building games has made me obsessive about interaction design in ways that no other medium could.

04

AutoCAD & Engineering

Precision matters. My background with AutoCAD and engineering systems taught me the discipline of exactness — measuring twice, committing once, and building structures that do not fail under load. That mindset shows up in how I architect software.

05

Project Management

I understand how projects succeed and how they fail. Timeline discipline, scope clarity, communication — these are not management abstractions. They are what separate a product that ships from one that does not. I bring this rigour to every engagement.

06

Creative Problem Solving

The most interesting problems do not have obvious solutions. My approach to problem solving — shaped by curiosity, cross-discipline thinking, and a refusal to accept the first answer — is what allows me to build things that other developers would not think to try.
